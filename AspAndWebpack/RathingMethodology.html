Introduction

The IHS Economics and Country Risk team scores risk numerically alongside its qualitative text-based forecasts. These scores are comparable across countries. They range from 0.1 to 10 with steps of 0.1 on a logarithmic scale, so a score increase from 8.1 to 8.2 represents a larger increase in risk than a move from 1.1 to 1.2. Each score represents the average expected level of risk over the next year. These indices are dynamic.

How scores are set and reviewed

IHS economists and country risk analysts score risks based on their own expert understanding of the countries' political, economic, social, and security environment, using economic models, information from open sources, and structured intelligence gathered by a network of thousands of in-country personnel. Daily written assessments highlight a country's changing conditions and inform the risk scores. In addition to in-house expertise and models, IHS draws on experts in the field and in-country sources with proven records of reliability. Our wide network of external analysts and contacts gives us access to local, specific, and granular information. The country risk scores also benefit from the insight and data generated for two other specialised IHS risk products – the Sovereign Risk Service and the Global Risk Service.

Analysts can move the scores at any time in light of significant events that signal changes in the risk environment. Event-driven risk score changes can reflect developments such as elections, significant violent attacks, or a change in government policies. Regional desk meetings take place every week to review any such developments and their impact on the scores. The regional desk also reviews scores for any given country on a rolling basis, at least quarterly, alongside a qualitative review of the written briefs, or production of quarterly economic forecasts.

IHS's dedicated Risk Center works with analysts to regularly review scores and relative country rankings. An independent quarterly global review is carried out by auditors from the Political Violence and Political Risk teams as well as the Risk Center experts, who examine the scores for contextual and statistical consistency in a regional and global context. This review is aided by statistical metrics set and run by the Risk Center. The regional desks hold a meeting with the Risk Center to review their region's scores comprehensively on an annual basis.

Political Risk

Government Instability

Reflects the risk that the central, sovereign, or highest political authority in a country, or the individuals or groups constituting it, will change in the next year. In most cases this will be a government (used as shorthand below). The risk of forced change (e.g. by a coup, popular uprising, civil war, vote of no confidence, or impeachment) scores considerably higher than change in the course of ordinary politics, for example, a scheduled election, voluntary change of cabinet, or internal party elections. (This category does not capture any impact on policy).

6.5 to 10 - The government is internally unstable and its authority is severely challenged by various groups and any political opposition. A disorderly overthrow of the government is highly likely in the next year, probably as a result of a violent coup, popular uprising or civil war, and quite possibly with some degree of international support.

4.4 to 6.4 - The government's authority is severely challenged domestically and maybe internationally. It faces a significant risk of being forcefully changed in the coming year. The change will not be orderly and those coming to power may have done so thanks to foreign support.

3.2 to 4.3 - Frequent, widespread, and increasingly bold challenges to the government's authority from civil society groups or political opposition are likely to force a change in government the next year. While forced, the change will be relatively orderly (e.g. a vote of no confidence) and will not alter the fundamental institutions of government significantly.

2.4 to 3.1 - The government may rely on holding a coalition together to stay in power, and this is becoming harder to do as its popularity falls and divisions emerge. There is therefore a growing risk that the government will be dissolved and elections called in the next year, outside the normal election cycle. A new party and leader has a significant chance of winning this election; OR Elections are due in the next year and opinion polls point firmly to a heavy defeat for the incumbent and a change of government.

1.6 to 2.3 - The government is fairly stable. Its authority faces occasional external challenges from civil society groups, either domestic- or foreign-based. The government may be removed from power by organised political opposition in the course of ordinary politics within the next year. Forced change, however, remains unlikely.

0.8 to 1.5 - The government is stable and does not face significant challenges to its authority domestically or internationally. There is a small chance of a change in government in the one-year outlook but this is likely to be in the course of ordinary politics in the country.

0.1 to 0.7 - The country's government is well-established, internally stable, well-supported domestically and respected internationally. There is little genuinely credible challenge to its authority (although perhaps to its policies) from any organised political opposition, civil society groups or powerful individuals. There is almost no chance of the government changing in the next year.

Policy Direction

The risk the government's broad policy framework shifts over the next year, making the business environment more challenging. This might include e.g. implementing more onerous employment or environmental regulation, local content requirements, import/export barriers, tariffs or quotas, other protectionist measures, price controls or caps, more 'political' control of monetary policy, or simply more direct intervention into the operations and decisions of private companies etc.

6.5 to 10 - The state is highly likely to make sudden, unilateral decisions that prevent businesses from operating efficiently or generating profits. These decisions are likely to curtail the ability of businesses across sectors from operating day-to-day or make strategic decisions. These decisions are likely to be sudden and directed from the national authority, rather than going through a debate or negotiation process with civil society or political groups.

4.4 to 6.4 - The state is highly likely to pass fundamental legislation extensively expanding its scope to intervene in the day-to-day operations and strategic decisions of private enterprises. This will accompany other policy initiatives likely to make it much harder for businesses to operate efficiently or generate profits. This highly likely to be in light of ongoing public debate, and supported by opposition political groups and influential civil society groups.

3.2 to 4.3 - In the next year, the state is likely to make significant changes to or pass new legislation affecting the operations of several sectors, or change fundamental legislation outlining the rights of state bodies to intervene in the strategic decisions of private enterprises. This highly likely to be in light of ongoing public debate, and supported by opposition political groups and influential civil society groups.

2.4 to 3.1 - Opposition political groups' and influential civil society bodies' criticism of state policy towards private enterprise represent broad public opinion. In response the state is likely to adjust its policies in the next year. In addition to regulatory changes, this may include new legislation in some sectors to expand the scope for state oversight of decisions by private enterprise.

1.6 to 2.3 - The state's policies affecting private enterprise are regularly challenged by opposition political parties in addition to civil society groups. Criticism is likely - but need not be - centred on the freedoms of foreign firms and/or investors. The state is likely to respond by adjusting some regulations but is unlikely to abandon its commitment to domestic and foreign private enterprise.

0.8 to 1.5 - State policies affecting private enterprise remain stable but are increasingly challenged in the specialised political discourse among influential think tanks and/ or segments of civil society. The state, however, remains unlikely to fundamentally alter its support of private enterprise as the driver of the national economy and is evidently keen to encourage both domestic and foreign businesses. Well-organised and influential think tanks and/ or civil society groups campaign for expanded state oversight, if not outright control, of day-to-day and strategic business decisions. This criticism, however, is not a central feature of the political opposition's platform.

0.1 to 0.7 - State policies towards private enterprise are stable or on an improving trend. There may be specific regulatory changes affecting particular sectors but business policy is consistently based on encouraging private enterprise (within the constraints of prudential regulation), with minimal state interference in day-to-day or strategic management and/ or discrimination between domestic and foreign firms. There is no significant political pressure from civil society or opposition political parties for change.

State Failure

The risk the state is unable exclusively to ensure public security and law and order, and the supply of basic goods like food, water, infrastructure and energy (if state provision of these is severely challenged or entirely supplanted by non-state actors, the state has failed). The state may be unable to respond to or manage current or likely future emergencies, including natural disasters and financial or economic crises. This may be due to inability to collect taxes to fund these activities, government paralysis or collapse.

6.5 to 10 - The country is effectively a failed state. Not only is state provision of basic security and public services severely curtailed or entirely supplanted by groups of non-state actors in most of the country, these groups bitterly compete against each other. Basic law and order is absent and state institutions are unable to respond. Organised criminal gangs may well have stepped into the power vacuum, and the country is suffering from e.g. extensive drug cultivation or trafficking, high homicide and kidnapping rates.

4.4. to 6.4 - The state can provide some basic services but probably only in the capital/its primary cities, and even this is contingent upon foreign support/aid. The central government's writ or tax raising powers do not extend to many parts of the country, where local strongmen/warlords effectively run operations and the rule of law.

3.2 to 4.3 - State institutions generally cannot respond effectively to emergencies/crises, perhaps because resources are already stretched thin, and have no significant planning to base action on in their event. The state's provision of basic security and public services is patchy and unreliable. In some parts of the country non-state actors, e.g. warlords or armed insurgents, openly challenge the state's provision of these goods and in some cases provide these services themselves. By doing so, these non-state actors may wield substantial informal political power, for example, by influencing election polling or contract terms in some areas.

2.4 to 3.1 - State institutions are moderately competent and generally funded enough to provide basic security and public services in most of the country, but often not effective. In some parts however, the state cannot be relied on for these goods. The state does not have the capacity to respond robustly to emergencies/crises either because of insufficient funding and/ or equipment, or a lack of reliable planning.

1.6 to 2.3 - State institutions' staffs are mostly competent but not always effective. State institutions are sufficiently funded and are capable of providing basic security and public services and also responding to most emergencies/crises but their response is likely to be hindered by confusion about their respective responsibilities. Crisis management and mitigation planning are not robust and are average compared internationally.

0.8 to 1.5 - The state has well-functioning civil institutions, with the funding and capacity to respond to both daily tasks and most sudden crises. There are some overlaps, however, in the responsibilities of different bodies of the state that are likely to complicate an adequate and clearly coordinated response in the event of an emergency/crisis. Crisis management and mitigation planning is broadly robust.

0.1 to 0.7 - State institutions are impressive by international standards, with well-functioning civil institutions that have the funding and capacity to provide all basic services and respond adequately to sudden crises. The responsibilities of different bodies of the state are clearly understood and day-to-day tasks are well coordinated between them. Crisis management and mitigation planning are well established and robust.

Bribery And Corruption

The risk that individuals will face demands for bribes or other corrupt practices in order to carry out business, from securing major contracts to being allowed to import/export a small product or obtain everyday paperwork. This can threaten a company's ability to operate in a country, or open it up to legal or regulatory penalties and reputational damage.

6.5 to 10 - Navigating demands for bribes and/or corrupt practices among various, competing patronage networks, even at the most senior levels of public and private sectors, is intrinsic to doing business in the country. Demands will be substantial and probably recurring. The progress of projects, and perhaps even physical safety, is contingent upon them. Anti-corruption legislation, if any, is very rarely enforced unless to persecute political or business rivals.

4.4 to 6.4 - Multiple patronage networks demand bribes and other corrupt practices and are often in competition with each other. Failure to cooperate with them puts contracts at risk. The networks are well-embedded in local business, bureaucratic, and political networks and their demands are fairly predictable. Businesses, especially those unfamiliar with local networks, typically have no legal recourse to demands for bribes and/or corrupt practices.

3.2 to 4.3 - Bribe-taking and/or corrupt practices are widespread in both public and private sectors and junior- and middle-levels of seniority. Many public officials will have had to pay bribes to secure their positions and now wish to 'capitalise' on their investment. Refusal to engage usually risks contracts. Firms without established local business networks generally use 'recommended' local subcontractors, even to secure basic services such as telephone connections and electricity. Most institutions are captured by local interests. Anti-corruption legislation is weak and/often arbitrarily enforced.

2.4 to 3.1 - Small bribes demands and/ or some corrupt practices are common from junior-level state bureaucrats for granting operating licences. Refusal to pay bribes and/or engage in corrupt practices sometimes leads to project cancellation. Bureaucratic procedures to obtain operating permits are inefficient and widely perceived as regularly abused. Requests for facilitation payments are likely in order to secure or expedite paperwork, and are pretty endemic at customs. Anti-corruption legislation is weak and/or inconsistently enforced.

1.6 to 2.3 - Bribes demands and/ or corrupt practices are typical but these tend to be relatively small and are generally restricted to particular sectors. Bureaucratic procedures for operating licences are inefficient and open to abuse. Refusal to pay bribes and/or engage in corrupt practices generally delays projects but typically does not lead to their cancellation. Anti-corruption legislation may be well-established but is not comprehensively enforced.

0.8 to 1.5 - There have been occasional well-publicised cases of businesses facing demands for bribes to enhance their commercial prospects in the last three years but such demands remain uncommon. The state bureaucracy is mostly efficient, with few opportunities to demand corrupt payments, but is not highly transparent by international standards. Businesses under pressure for bribes and/or corrupt practices have recourse to well-established anti-corruption legislation that is robustly enforced.

0.1 to 0.7 - Demands for bribes are very rare. Discrimination by regulatory authorities, state bureaucrats, or private counterparties in the awarding of business on any grounds other than business merit is also very rare. Bureaucratic procedures to obtain operating licences and permits are transparent and efficient, with few opportunities to demand corrupt payments.

Infrastructure Risk

Infrastructural Disruption

This is the likelihood of disruption to and/or inadequacy of infrastructure for transport, including due to e.g. terrorism/insurgency, strikes, politically motivated shutdowns, natural disasters; infrastructure includes (as relevant): roads, railways, airports, ports, and customs check points.

6.5 to 10 - The country has so little or such dilapidated infrastructure that is so often disrupted that commercial activities are practically impossible.

4.4 to 6.4 - The country has infrastructure that is either severely insufficient or inefficient and is prone to significant and constant disruption, with a particular impact on commercial activities (e.g. prevents access to major commercial centres, shuts down significant thoroughfares, closes down borders).

3.2 to 4.3 - The country has infrastructure that is either highly insufficient or inefficient that is prone to significant and constant disruption OR the country has infrastructure that is either moderately insufficient or inefficient but that is likely to disrupt commercial activities (e.g. disrupts access to major commercial centres, blocks significant thoroughfares temporarily, significantly slows down border crossings).

2.4 to 3.1 - The country has infrastructure that is either highly insufficient or inefficient that is prone to disruption of up to days or weeks at a time, but disruption to commercial activities somewhat mitigated.

1.6 to 2.3 - The country has infrastructure that is either moderately insufficient or inefficient that is prone to disruption of up to days at a time OR the country has a comprehensive and efficient infrastructure, but it is prone to disruption of up to weeks at a time.

0.8 to 1.5 - The country has a comprehensive and efficient infrastructure, but it is prone to disruption of up to days at a time.

0.1 to 0.7 - The country has a comprehensive and efficient infrastructure, that either does not face threat of disruption or has robust mitigation strategies in place to address disruptive forces quickly.

0.1 to 0.7 - There is only occasional industrial action and usually with significant contingency provided by the state to minimise disruption. The labour force is well represented by recognised unions that are able to engage employers and state institutions in structured dialogue. Working conditions and employment protections are very good and wage inequality between industrial sectors is low by international standards. The economy is stable and growing and most households do not face financial pressures.

Aviation

The risk (probability and impact) of: state or non-state armed groups targeting aircraft in the airspace/airspace claimed by the country in question or related airports infrastructure; protest groups either damaging or causing disruption to these assets; the government seizing or confiscating aviation assets. NB: issues like delays due to inefficient infrastructure and poor management should be reflected in the 'Infrastructure Disruption' score.

6.5 to 10 - As a result of inter-state or civil war or other conflicts, the one-year outlook features widespread areas of strategic political and economic importance where both military and civil cargo aircraft come under attack, in the air and on the ground, most days. The attackers will probably have demonstrated the capability to engage aircraft using man portable air defence systems (MANPADS), if not high-altitude surface-to-air missiles, as well as with low-level air defence (heavy machine guns). Heavy weapons (typically, Grad-type rockets, mortars) or vehicle-borne improvised explosive devices (VBIED) will most days cause major targeted or collateral damage to airport infrastructure and/or cargo, shutting aviation operations down for months at a time. Aircraft and aviation cargo will be subject to arbitrary seizure and/or theft, both by state and non-state actors. (NB: A score of 7.5 and above should be reserved for use when there is a CBRN threat capable of inflicting city-wide property damage and shutting down aviation operations.)

4.4 to 6.4 - As a result of inter-state, civil war or insurgency, the one-year outlook features several areas of strategic political and economic importance where both military and civil cargo aircraft come under attack, in the air and on the ground, most weeks. The attackers will have the capability to engage aircraft in the air using low-level air defence weapons and small-arms and are very likely to have access to MANPADS and aspire to acquire high-altitude surface-to-air missiles. On the ground, aviation infrastructure will be targeted by heavy weapons (typically, Grad-type rockets, mortars) and ground attack using vehicle-borne improvised explosive devices (VBIED). Airport operations are likely to be shut down for at least a week at a time. Aircraft and aviation cargo will be subject to arbitrary seizure by state and/or theft by non-state actors.

3.2 to 4.3 - Probably as a result of an insurgency, the one-year outlook features at least one identified, economically significant area where both military and civil cargo aircraft come under attack, in the air and on the ground, most months. Weapons used against aircraft in the air will include low-level air defence (heavy machine guns). Attackers are likely to have limited access to older MANPADS or aspire to acquire them and there is an identifiable and feasible source of such weapons.� Airport infrastructure and/or cargo will be at collateral risk, if not targeted attack, from heavy weapons (typically, Grad-type rockets, mortars) and ground attack using vehicle-borne improvised explosive devices (VBIEDs). Airport operations are likely to be shut down for up to several days at a time. Aircraft and aviation cargo belonging to particular countries/commercial carriers will be at risk of arbitrary seizure by state and/or theft by non-state actors.

2.4 to 3.1 - Probably as a result of an insurgency or 'for profit' organised crime, the one-year outlook features at least one economically significant area where aircraft come under regular (around quarterly) attack, in the air and/or on the ground. Alternatively, an armed confrontation, in which at least one party has access to high-altitude surface-to-air missiles, creates a low probability/high impact risk of shoot-down through misidentification, even if the risk of civil or interstate war is assessed as low. Security measures in place in the area(s) exposed to violent risk at domestic, if not international airports, are likely to be ineffective in preventing militants from attacking airport infrastructure, but they are unlikely to penetrate security to reach aircraft on the ground.� Targeted attacks or collateral damage to aircraft/aviation cargo are likely to cause no more than minor damage or a day's disruption to aviation operations. Aircraft and aviation cargo belonging to particular countries/commercial carriers will be at risk of arbitrary seizure, primarily by state actors

1.6 to 2.3 - The one-year outlook features identified 'hotspots' where the level of violent risk posed by international or domestic terrorism, 'for profit' crime or violent unrest is higher than the assessed country risk score. Military, if not civil, aircraft are a likely aspirational terrorist shoot-down/IED target but, even in the 'hotspots' intent is not matched by attack capability. Targeted attacks on/collateral damage to aircraft on the ground /airport infrastructure are infrequent (once a year or less) and unlikely to cause even minor damage or a day's disruption to aviation operations. There is an increased risk of state shootdown of small aircraft suspected of involvement in drug trafficking in certain regions. Outside the 'hotspots', civil aviation and aviation cargo being used for lawful business are unlikely to be seized by state authorities or stolen by organised crime or terrorists.

0.8 to 1.5 - The country's overall business operating environment is generally benign and security measures in place are assessed as effective and proportionate to the risk levels posed by terrorism, organised crime and violent unrest. Targeted attacks on/collateral damage to aircraft or airport infrastructure are rare (once in last five years), if they have occurred at all. Occasional civil unrest or industrial action will likely disrupt land cargo operations for a few days at a time. Civil aviation and aviation cargo on lawful business face a low risk of official seizure and when this occurs due process is followed.

0.1 to 0.7 - The country's business operating environment is benign and security measures in place are assessed as effective and proportionate to the risk levels posed by terrorism, organised crime and violent unrest. Attacks on/collateral damage to aircraft or airport infrastructure are virtually unknown. Civil unrest or industrial action will likely disrupt access to some airports for up to a few days at a time. Civil aircraft and aviation cargo on lawful business face a negligible risk of seizure; if this occurs due process will be followed.

Ground

The risk (probability and impact) that ground (road and rail) cargo and/or passengers, along with related transport infrastructure (e.g. rail stations, lorry depots, bus stations) will experience damage and/or delay as a result of war, terrorism or unrest, or face government seizure/confiscation NB: issues like delays due to inefficient infrastructure and poor management should be reflected in the 'Infrastructure Disruption' score.

6.5 to 10 - As a result of inter-state or civil war, the one-year outlook features widespread areas of strategic economic and political importance where land cargo and transportation come under attack, or their operations are disrupted, most days. Heavy weapons (typically, heavy machine guns, Grad-type rockets, mortars) and sophisticated improvised explosive devices (IED) will frequently cause major targeted/collateral damage to land cargo and transportation, shutting down cargo operations for weeks at a time. Land cargo and transportation will be subject to arbitrary seizure and confiscation, both by state and non-state actors. (NB: A score of 7.5 and above should be reserved for use when there is a CBRN threat capable of inflicting city-wide property damage.)

4.4 to 6.4 -As a result of inter-state, civil war or insurgency, the one-year outlook features several areas of strategic economic and political importance where both land cargo and transportation come under attack, or their operations are disrupted, most weeks. Heavy weapons (typically, heavy machine guns Grad-type rockets, mortars) and sophisticated IEDs will regularly (most months) cause severe targeted/collateral damage to land cargo and transportation, shutting down their operations for at least a week at a time. Cargo and vehicles are subject to arbitrary seizure and confiscation, both by state and non-state actors.

3.2 to 4.3 -Probably as a result of an insurgency, or 'for profit' organised crime, the one-year outlook features at least one well-identified/economically-significant area where both land cargo and transportation come under attack, or their operations are disrupted, most months. In an insurgency, heavy weapons (typically, heavy machine guns, Grad-type rockets, mortars) or sophisticated IED will regularly cause severe targeted/collateral damage to land cargo and transportation, shutting down their operations for up to several days at a time. Cargo and vehicles are subject to arbitrary seizure and confiscation, both by state and non-state actors.

2.4 to 3.1 - Probably as a result of an insurgency or 'for profit' organised crime, the one-year outlook includes at least one economically significant area where military vehicles or land cargo is targeted, or is at risk of suffering collateral damage or operational disruption, through fighting or civil unrest. Such incidents take place about once a quarter, shutting down cargo operations for up to a few days at a time. The weapons used by the militants or 'for profit' criminals will consist mainly of small arms and RPGs. In the high risk area, cargo and vehicles will be subject to seizure by non-state actors.

1.6 to 2.3 - The one-year outlook features identified 'hotspots' where the level of violent risk to land cargo and transportation posed by terrorism, violent unrest and/or 'for profit' crime is higher than the assessed country risk score. Land cargo and transportation in the 'hotspot' areas are likely aspirational targets but intent is not fully matched by attack capability. Targeted attacks on/collateral damage to land cargo or transportation are infrequent (once a year or less) and unlikely to cause even minor damage or a day's disruption to operations. The weapons used by the militants or 'for profit' criminals will consist mainly of small arms, including rocket-propelled grenades (RPG) and rudimentary IEDs. Land cargo and transportation on lawful business face a low risk of confiscation and when this occurs due process is followed.

0.8 to 1.5 - The country's business operating environment is generally benign. Targeted attacks on/collateral damage on land cargo or transportation are rare (once in last five years), if they have occurred at all. Occasional disruption caused by violent unrest/industrial action leads to delays for a few days, at the most. Land cargo and transportation on lawful business face a negligible risk of confiscation; if this occurs due process will be followed.

0.1 to 0.7 - The country's business operating environment is benign and overall security measures are assessed as effective and proportionate to the risk levels posed by terrorism, violent unrest and/or organised crime. Targeted attacks on/collateral damage to land cargo or transportation are virtually unknown. Land cargo and transportation on lawful business face a negligible risk of confiscation and when this occurs due process is followed.

Marine

The risk (probability and impact) of: state or non-state armed groups targeting vessels in the territorial waters/waters claimed by the country in question, related ports infrastructure and any other offshore assets; protest groups either damaging or causing disruption to these assets; the government seizing or confiscating marine assets. NB: issues like delays due to inefficient infrastructure and poor management should be reflected in the 'Infrastructure Disruption' score.

6.5 to 10 - As a result of inter-state or civil war, the one-year outlook features targeted attacks, or collateral damage to, commercial vessels, marine cargo and port operations most days, to the point that damage to all key ports and disruption to shipping brings commercial operations to a standstill for months at a time. At least one side in the conflict will have an effective anti-ship missile capability. Vessels and marine cargo are subject to arbitrary seizure and/or theft, both by state and non-state actors. (NB: A score of 7.5 and above should be reserved for use when there is a CBRN threat capable of inflicting city-wide property damage, shutting down marine operations.)

4.4 to 6.4 - As a result of inter-state, civil war or insurgency, the one-year outlook features targeted attacks on, or collateral damage to, commercial vessels and marine cargo most weeks, to the point that damage to at least one key port and disruption to shipping brings commercial operations to a standstill for at least a week at a time. Ashore, port infrastructure will be targeted by heavy weapons (typically, Grad-type rockets, mortars) and ground attack using vehicle-borne improvised explosive devices (VBIEDs).At least one side in the conflict will have the capability to target ships in port, if not offshore. Vessels and marine cargo will be subject to arbitrary seizure and/or theft, both by non-state actors.

3.2 to 4.3 - Probably as a result of an insurgency or 'for profit' organised crime, the one-year outlook features at least one economically significant area of the coast or territorial, if not adjacent international, waters, where military or commercial shipping is targeted, or is at risk of suffering collateral damage or operational disruption through fighting or civil unrest onshore or piracy (international waters) or sea robbery (territorial waters). Such attacks take place most months. In the case of an insurgency, at least one side in the conflict will have the capability to target ships in port, if not offshore. Vessels belonging to particular countries or commercial carriers are at high risk of arbitrary seizure and confiscation/theft, both by state and non-state actors.

2.4 to 3.1 - Probably as a result of an insurgency or 'for profit' organised crime, the one-year outlook includes at least one economically significant area of the coast or territorial, if not adjacent international, waters, where military or commercial shipping is targeted, or is at risk of suffering collateral damage or operational disruption through fighting or civil unrest onshore or piracy (international waters) or sea robbery (territorial waters). Such incidents take place about once a quarter. Alternatively, an armed confrontation, in which at least one party has access to anti-shipping missiles, creates a low probability/high impact risk of engagement through misidentification, even if civil or interstate war remains a low probability. Security measures in place at ports in the affected area(s) are unlikely to be fully effective in mitigating the threat.

1.6 to 2.3 - The one-year outlook features identified 'hotspots' where the level of violent risk posed shipping and/or port operations by terrorism, 'for profit' crime or violent unrest is higher than the assessed country risk score. Commercial shipping and/or warships are a likely aspirational terrorist engagement/IED target but intent is not matched by attack capability. Targeted attacks on/collateral damage to ships in port/airport infrastructure are infrequent (once a year or less) and unlikely to cause even minor damage or a day's disruption to marine operations. Commercial shipping and marine cargo are unlikely to be seized or confiscated providing local regulations are respected; when this occurs (e.g. in disputed waters) due process is followed.

0.8 to 1.5 - The country's overall business operating environment is generally benign and security measures in place are assessed as effective and proportionate to the risk levels posed by terrorism, organised crime and violent unrest. Targeted attacks on, or collateral damage to, shipping or port infrastructure are rare (once in last five years), if they have occurred at all. Any disruption to marine operations as a result of unrest or industrial action is likely to be infrequent (once a year or less) and very unlikely to cause damage. Any disruption/delay to marine operations is unlikely to last more than several days. Commercial shipping and marine cargo on lawful business face a low risk of seizure; when this occurs due process is followed.

0.1 to 0.7 - The country's business operating environment is benign and security measures are assessed as effective and proportionate to the risk levels posed by terrorism, organised crime and violent unrest. Attacks on/collateral damage to commercial shipping and marine cargo are virtually unknown. Major disruption/delay at ports by civil unrest or industrial action is rare (less than once a year) and leads to delays of a few days at the most. Commercial shipping and marine cargo on lawful business face a negligible risk of seizure; if this occurs, due process will be followed.

Internal Security Risk

Protests and Riots

The likelihood, nature and impact of protests and riots (excluding those related to labour), particularly if these disrupt normal movement, operations and activity, cause damage to assets or injure or detain people. The nature and impact of the state's response should also be factored in here.

6.5 to 10 - Civil groups are challenging the state and looking to overthrow the government, regularly amassing several hundred thousand protesters for protests and riots lasting more than two weeks at a time. The state loses control of major population centres which become 'no-go' areas for security forces. Security forces are authorised to use lethal force; their response can itself cause property damage and death/injury, and serve to escalate rather than de-escalate levels of unrest. Some protesters may cause property damage and/or injury through the use of arson, small IEDs and small-arms fire. Certain assets (e.g. government or those associated with a particular political party or nationality etc.) may be singled out and subjected to deliberate and significant damage. Certain groups of people (based on political affiliations, ethnicity, nationality etc.) may be singled out for temporary detention, severe beatings or even killing, with high risks also for bystanders. A large proportion of business activity is halted for weeks at a time and state services (including transport, power, communication, health and sanitation) are severely curtailed. EXAMPLES: Egypt anti-Morsi, Ukraine Maidan protests

4.4 to 6.4 - Civil groups are challenging the government but fall short of threatening its survival. They nonetheless amass tens of thousands of protesters in key cities for protests and riots lasting up to a week. Protests often turn into riots with significant damage to property from thrown projectiles and arson with high death and injury risk especially from a heavy handed security force response. Business activity tends to be halted for the duration of the unrest. OR; there is a moderate likelihood that the unrest will lead to a populist response by the government, leading to major concessions like the cancelation or at least very lengthy/costly delays to controversial laws, projects etc. EXAMPLES: Egypt mid-2014

3.2 to 4.3 - There are very frequent (monthly), large-scale protests that disrupt business activity and/or transport networks in key cities for several days at a time and cause some limited property damage in city centres OR; occasional large-scale violent protests or riots causing disruption for several days and moderate damage to property in smaller cities / larger towns or rural areas (occasionally at main facilities but usually peripheral assets or vehicles outside the main perimeter). Death/injury risks tend to be limited to protest participants and the security forces, but with moderate risks to bystanders.

2.4 to 3.1 - Frequent (several times a year) large-scale organised protests that cause some disruption to transport and business but rarely last more than a week; occasional riots with some vandalism, including damage to shop fronts and some looting OR; occasional large-scale violent protests or riots causing damage to property in affected areas and/or severe disruption to those specific projects targeted by protesters and the immediate vicinity.

1.6 to 2.3 - Regular smaller-scale non-violent protests (fewer than 10,000 people) that cause some disruption to business activity but rarely last longer than 2-3 days - occasionally there is some vandalism during protests but this is very limited; OR; Regular but small-scale (fewer than 10,000 people) violent protests in rural areas.

0.8 to 1.5 - There are some issues that trigger protests on a regular but infrequent basis and protests tend to be orderly and non-violent. Riots are very rare and happen less than once a year - when they do the police response tends to be effective in containing violence.

0.1 to 0.7 - There are very few organised protests and these are almost never violent.

Terrorism

The activities of any non-state armed group or individual causing, or likely to cause, property damage and/or death/injury through violence. Violent risks posed by organised criminal groups (extortion-related IEDs and arson, shootings etc.) should be factored in here. Although insurgency is also covered under 'Civil War', this should be considered at the high end of this terrorism risk score scale. Likewise, it's OK that there's overlap with our 'Death and Injury' score.

6.5 to 10 - Major attacks (large VBIEDs, rockets etc.) against a range of buildings, transport and public places occur across much of the country most days as the state confronts numerous, well-armed, highly motivated and organised insurgents. Loss of life and property damage is significant and includes the complete destruction of assets. NB: A score above 7.5 must be used if terrorists possess, or are close to acquiring, a deployable CBRN capability able to inflict mass civilian casualties (1,000+) and/or city-wide property damage (such as a tactical nuclear device). Aside from an actual attack, security forces' disruption of developed attack plans or evidence, not just of terrorist intent, but of developing technical capability/theft of the materials needed would be indicators justifying this score. EXAMPLES: Iraq

4.4 to 6.4 - Terrorist groups conduct large-scale bomb attacks causing complete or partial destruction of assets in both urban and rural locations killing more than 10 people per incident most weeks; the state's security apparatus is very inadequate to meet the threat. OR; in countries with active insurgent groups, significant attacks on security forces, government assets and private business assets occur several times a week and can regularly lead to complete disruption to business activities in affected areas and significant population displacement. OR; organised criminal groups conduct several major VBIED attacks per month, causing major property damage and loss of life (must be roughly comparable with Pablo Escobar in Colombia late 80s/early 90s). EXAMPLES: Afghanistan, Nigeria

3.2 to 4.3 - There is a significant terrorism problem, with successful attacks at least every one or two months causing deaths and some property damage. However, attacks tend to be focussed on specific geographies or sectors rather than be truly nationwide. OR; capable domestic actors have a clear agenda to bring down the government by targeting sensitive assets and their attacks succeed regularly (at least every one or two months). OR; multiple organised criminal groups use small-scale IEDs, arson or shootings at least several times per month; these tend to be directed at direct criminal rivals or security forces but can also inflict major damage and / or death/injury to those refusing to pay extortion. Large-scale gun battles between rival criminal groups are commonplace, including in public places in urban centres (especially if frequented by our client set or those they are responsible for).

2.4 to 3.1 - There are frequent (at least every other month), low capability bomb attacks (partial property destruction), small-arms attacks or fighting with security forces that can disrupt business activity. OR; the country is a proven or likely target for attacks by regional or international groups with proven capability and reach. Occasional attacks causing mass casualties and large-scale property destruction are likely, although less frequently than once a year. OR; organised criminal groups or single-issue activists carrying out fairly frequent (monthly) small-scale IED or arson attacks but these tend to be confined to a narrow set of targets and geographies and rarely cause major property damage or injury. (Be careful not to inflate: incidents like the 2009 Athens Stock Exchange IED are relatively significant for Greece, but we'd need to believe such attacks will become the norm or even expand in order to justify a score of >3.1). OR; Although the extremely high homicide-per-capita rate is primarily driven by inter-gang shootings, innocent bystanders are occasionally injured/killed.

1.6 to 2.3 - There are domestic armed groups that actively target assets or people in some parts of the country but with limited capability (pipe bombs and other small explosive devices, small-scale arson, vandalism). OR; the country is an aspirational target for jihadis or other extremist groups (though most indicators suggest sizeable attacks will be very rare - less than every three years). OR; there are numerous organised crime groups and gangs that fight each other and security forces but rarely target property or uninvolved civilians; collateral harm to bystanders occurs occasionally (a few times per year) but is unlikely to become the norm.

0.8 to 1.5 - There have been very occasional incidents of low capability bomb attacks (non-lethal and very limited property damage), arson, foiled plots or the arrest of suspects. In general, threats from domestic terrorist groups are very constrained and primarily limited to small-scale vandalism. Even if the country features on jihadist and other extremist websites there is little to suggest a successful attack will materialise in the coming year.

0.1 to 0.7 - There is little to no terrorism threat and scant precedent. Any domestic actors with political or other grievances are unlikely to resort to terrorism and if they did, their capability would be minimal. The country is not a prominent target on websites of jihadists or other groups.

Internal Conflict (known in ECR as Civil WAR)

The likelihood and impact of intra-state military conflict (in the form of an organised insurgency, separatist conflict or full-blown civil war where rebels/insurgents are attempting to overthrow the government, achieve regional independence or at least heavily influence major government policies).

*NB: Some definitions of civil war use casualty rates (e.g. >1,000 in total or per year) to distinguish them from insurgency/terrorism. Some insist the state/government must be one of the parties in an internal conflict; however, this would mean we'd exclude things like Sunni v Shia militias in Iraq, violence between rival militias in Libya or between the various rebels groups within Syria, Tutsi v Hutu in DRC etc. Although by no means definitive, we will use the rule of thumb that the non-state armed group(s) in question would normally have a relatively organised military force, exercise de facto authority over the population within a determinate portion of the national territory and have an organisation purporting to have the characteristics of a state (borrowing from the ICRC's definition). Violence by such groups should feature in our analysis and risk scores for both civil war and terrorism. However, any violence by groups which do not broadly meet the criteria above should feature in terrorism only.

6.5 to 10 - There is either an ongoing large-scale civil war or one will almost certainly erupt in the one-year outlook. Military forces are deployed in sustained operations in a significant part of the country and fighting involves the regular use of air strikes, medium/long range missiles and armour and/or field artillery, targeting strategic infrastructure, transport hubs, population centres and commercial assets alongside security forces. Outside actors may be providing significant and ongoing 'game changing' backing for one or more of the belligerents, e.g. via supplies of advanced weapons systems, financing, political legitimisation, training, military advice, safe haven, covert asymmetrical operations etc. Meaningful negotiations or a peacekeeping mission to end the civil war are very unlikely. There is significant property destruction and damage on a daily basis arising from the use of air strikes and/or heavy weapons, and most business activity is severely disrupted. Targeting often appears indiscriminate, with collateral victims the norm. NB: For any score above 7.5, CBRN weapons are available to main protagonists and are at a real risk of being used. EXAMPLES: Syria, Iraq

4.4 to 6.4 - There is either an ongoing civil war or one will likely erupt in the one-year outlook. Military forces are deployed in operations in some parts of the country and fighting involves some use of air strikes, medium/long range missiles and armour and/or field artillery, targeting strategic infrastructure, transport hubs, population centres and commercial assets alongside security forces. Outside actors may be providing occasional yet substantial backing to one or more of the belligerents, which serves to significantly prolong the conflict and can result in periodic spikes in violence, long-term geographical shifts in the conflict and more ambitious targeting for prolonged periods. There is regular property destruction and damage arising from the use of air strikes and/or heavy weapons and regular disruption to business activity in some areas but several key cities have functioning services. Although some strikes may be clinical in nature, collateral victims are very common. Rebels/insurgents may often resort to asymmetrical tactics like major VBIEDs in urban centres, often targeting government/security force buildings or clusters of rival civilian groups; collateral property damage and harm to individuals is common in such cases.

3.2 to 4.3 - Despite the absence of a full-blown civil war, there is a significant ongoing campaign against insurgents in parts of the country or this is likely in the one-year outlook. Protagonists use primarily small-arms, including RPGs, machine guns and mortars, although there can be occasional use of field artillery, armour and anti-aircraft weapons. Air strikes are uncommon but occasional. Most fighting is primarily in rural areas rather than key cities and commercial hubs. Nevertheless, such urban locations are occasionally targeted by VBIEDs (mostly government/security force targets but with significant collateral risks). Some insurgent attacks target key infrastructure and rural commercial assets (e.g. power or energy). Outside actors may be providing occasional and limited support for one or more of the belligerents, allowing them to hold ground they would otherwise lose but probably not sufficient to take new ground or regularly undertake more ambitious targeting.

2.4 to 3.1 - There is a limited insurgency being fought against state security forces in parts of the country. Fighting with insurgents primarily involves the use of small-arms, including RPGs, machine guns and mortars. Fighting is contained to rural areas and cities in the affected region mostly operate without disruption. There is some damage to infrastructure in the affected region and to some smaller population centres. Support from outside actors is important for the belligerents but is probably not sufficient to allow them to take new ground or expand their normal target sets.

1.6 to 2.3 - Groups exist with a separatist agenda but have shunned/been excluded from mainstream politics. Their radical fringes occasionally mount protests, advocate insurgency (openly or more covertly via graffiti, social media etc) or even make tentative efforts at securing funding/training/political backing for such. Very crude IEDs may occasionally be detonated, causing only minor damage and rarely injuries; however, there is little risk of civil war emerging in the one-year outlook. EXAMPLES: UK (Northern Ireland)

0.8 to 1.5 - Historical grievances (separatist, ethnic, political etc) exist, but tend to be aired only occasionally (e.g. during election campaigning or local-level squabbles) and may lead to rare cases of isolated killings. However, there is unlikely to be traction for even a rudimentary insurgency or separatist conflict in the one-year outlook.

0.1 to 0.7 - Typical grievances that would normally act as drivers for a separatist conflict or insurgency do not exist.

In the case of inter- or intra-state war, fighting involves regular use of air strikes, medium/long range missiles and armour and/or field artillery, targeting strategic infrastructure, transport hubs, population centres; AND/OR very large IEDs are used several times per week, causing significant deaths and injury. 0.8 to 1.5 - Kidnappings are likely to be very rare (less than once a year) and/or rarely target foreigners/locals; kidnappings are never violent and almost never take place in cities; 'express' kidnapping incidents are fairly common.

Labour Strikes

The likelihood, nature and impact of strikes, any form of industrial action (e.g. work to rule, go-slow, overtime ban, sit-in/occupation) or labour-related protests, particularly if these disrupt normal movement, operations and activity, cause damage to assets or injure or detain people. 6.5 to 10 - The majority of business activity is often severely disrupted by labour protests, and state services (including transport, power, communication, health and sanitation) are severely curtailed by protest activity. Strikes and labour-related protests and other forms of industrial action are sustained for more than three weeks and recur regularly, often turning violent. Violence can include significant damage to main facilities and injury or even death to non-striking staff or bystanders. A country may score at the higher end of this band if the state's response is highly likely to significantly exacerbate / enflame the situation directly (e.g. very heavy-handed policing or military deployment causing injury to bystanders and/or collateral damage).

4.4 to 6.4 - There is frequent, prolonged nationwide industrial action in key sectors that closes down or severely disrupts business activity for over two weeks at a time and often turns violent at strike sites. Non-striking staff or bystanders face high risks of injury and / or of being detained temporarily. State services (including transport, power, communication, health and sanitation) can be curtailed periodically by strikes / industrial action. Peripheral assets or vehicles outside the main perimeter face property damage and/or non-striking staff or bystanders face moderate risks of injury and / or of being detained temporarily. Trade unions actively encourage some level of violence. A country may score at the higher end of this band if the state's response is likely to exacerbate / enflame the situation directly (e.g. heavy-handed policing causing injury to bystanders or employees, which becomes a new protest grievance).

2.4 to 3.1 - There is regular industrial action in key sectors, although only occasionally involving multiple sectors at the same time or lasting longer than a week. Action may be initiated by representative unions but they either tacitly approve some violence or are unable to control all strikers.

1.6 to 2.3 - There is regular industrial action in key sectors but rarely lasting longer than three days or involving more than one sector at a time. Violence is mostly limited to minor scuffles between security forces and strikers.

0.8 to 1.5 - Grievances about working conditions and wages usually occur only when households face financial pressures and are isolated to particular sectors and typically resolved amicably through dialogue between representative trade bodies, employers, and state institutions.
